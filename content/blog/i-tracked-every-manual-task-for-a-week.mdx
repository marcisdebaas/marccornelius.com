I logged every repetitive task I did for five days straight. The results were uncomfortable — and extremely useful.

## The experiment

For one full work week, I kept a running log of every task I did that felt manual, repetitive, or like something a system could handle. I noted the task, how long it took, and whether it required my judgment or was purely mechanical. By Friday, the spreadsheet had 47 entries.

## What I found

Roughly 11 hours of my week went to tasks that didn't require my brain. Copy-pasting data between tools. Formatting reports. Sending the same three types of emails with slightly different names. Checking on deliverables that could have been tracked automatically. Eleven hours. That's more than a full workday spent on work that shouldn't need a human.

## The uncomfortable part

The hardest thing wasn't finding the tasks — it was admitting I'd been doing them for months without questioning it. Some of these processes started as quick one-off things that somehow became permanent fixtures. I never stopped to ask whether they still needed to exist in their current form.

## What I did about it

I picked the five most time-consuming items and built automations for three of them within two weeks. Report generation went from 45 minutes to zero — it runs itself every Monday now. Client status emails are templated and triggered automatically. Data entry between our CRM and project tool is synced.

## The takeaway

You don't know how much time you're wasting until you measure it. Try tracking your manual tasks for even two days. You'll find at least one thing that makes you think, "Why am I still doing this?" That's your starting point.
