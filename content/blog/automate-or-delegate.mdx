Not everything should be automated, and not everything should be handed to someone else. Here's a simple framework for deciding.

## The two options aren't the same

Automation and delegation both free up your time, but they solve different problems. Automation is best for tasks that are predictable, rule-based, and high-frequency. Delegation is best for tasks that require judgment, nuance, or human interaction. Mixing these up leads to frustration — either you automate something that needed a human, or you pay a human to do something a script could handle.

## The decision framework

Ask two questions about any task you want off your plate. First: does this task follow the same steps every time? If yes, it's a strong candidate for automation. Second: does this task require judgment or adaptation? If yes, delegate it. Some tasks fall in between — that's where you automate the predictable parts and delegate the rest.

## When to automate

Data entry. Report generation. Notifications and reminders. File organization. Status updates. Invoice creation. Anything that follows an if-this-then-that logic. These tasks don't get better when a human does them — they just get slower and more error-prone.

## When to delegate

Client communication that requires tone and empathy. Creative work that needs iteration. Strategic decisions that depend on context. Quality checks that require expertise. These tasks benefit from human judgment, and automating them usually produces mediocre results.

## The hybrid approach

The best systems combine both. A VA handles client emails, but a template library and auto-tagging system makes them twice as fast. Reports generate automatically, but a human adds the executive summary. The goal isn't to remove humans from every process — it's to make sure humans are doing the parts that actually need them.
